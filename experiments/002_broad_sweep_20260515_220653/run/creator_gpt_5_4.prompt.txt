# BenchBench research notes

Last researched: 2026-05-15

## Core take

BenchBench is a good idea if it judges whether a model can produce a *valid, useful, hard benchmark package*, not merely whether it can invent tasks that current models fail.

The major lesson from existing evals is that a benchmark is not just a prompt set. The best ones combine:

- a clear construct claim: what capability is being measured
- defensible item sourcing: expert-written, real-world, live, adversarial, or executable
- reliable scoring: objective answers, test harnesses, calibrated judges, or human preference aggregation
- human baselines: who can solve it and at what rate
- contamination controls: hidden/private splits, live refresh, new items, or provenance checks
- statistical analysis: discriminability, ranking stability, confidence intervals, item-level data
- update process: versioning, review, retirement of bad items, and fresh hard items as models improve

For BenchBench, the "frontier model cannot beat it by 50%" rule should be framed against humans, not absolute failure:

> A generated benchmark is frontier-resistant if qualified humans reach a high minimum score, while the best frontier solver reaches less than 50% of human performance under a fixed budget.

That avoids rewarding impossible, ambiguous, or ungradeable tasks.

## Benchmarks and what they actually do

### Broad knowledge and exam-style benchmarks

| Benchmark | What it tests | How it is built | How it scores | BenchBench lesson |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| MMLU | Broad multitask academic/professional knowledge | Multiple-choice questions across many subjects | Accuracy | Breadth makes adoption easy, but static exam-style datasets saturate and invite contamination. |
| MMLU-Pro | Harder multitask understanding and reasoning | Removes trivial/noisy MMLU items, adds harder reasoning questions, expands answer options from 4 to 10 | Accuracy, with prompt-stability analysis | A "better benchmark" can be a cleaned, harder, more stable version of a saturated one. BenchBench should reward item cleanup and prompt robustness, not just novelty. |
| GPQA | Graduate-level science Q&A | 448 multiple-choice questions written by biology, physics, and chemistry experts; designed to be hard even with web access | Accuracy; compares experts, skilled non-experts, and models | Expert construction plus human baselines are central. "Google-proof" is a useful property for generated benchmarks too. |
| Humanity's Last Exam | Expert-level closed-ended academic questions | 2,500 expert-authored multimodal questions across 100+ subjects; unambiguous, verifiable answers; designed against quick retrieval | Accuracy/calibration on closed-ended questions | Pre-screen against current models and require known, verifiable answers. This is close to BenchBench's desired benchmark object. |
| FrontierMath | Advanced math reasoning | Hundreds of original math problems crafted and vetted by expert mathematicians; some hidden/private | Exact answer or proof-style verification depending on problem | Originality and expert vetting matter, but very hard expert items need independent QA because grading/math errors become fatal. |

Sources: [MMLU](https://arxiv.org/abs/2009.03300), [MMLU-Pro](https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.01574), [GPQA](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.12022), [Humanity's Last Exam](https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.14249), [FrontierMath](https://arxiv.org/abs/2411.04872), [Epoch FrontierMath page](https://epoch.ai/frontiermath/the-benchmark).

## The eval-correlation material

I found two relevant recent correlation items.

### 1. OpenThoughts3 eval meta-analysis

OpenThoughts3 released results from evaluating about 1,000 models and shows a benchmark-correlation heatmap across reasoning benchmarks. Their main observations:

- rankings across different reasoning benchmarks generally positively correlate
- correlations are stronger within categories, such as math with math or code with code
- some pairs show strong agreement, such as AIME24 and AIME25
- some correlations hide saturation, such as easier math benchmarks where strong models cluster near perfect scores
- Pearson score correlation and Kendall rank correlation can tell different stories, especially when middle-tier models are hard to distinguish
- aggregate rankings can change when the benchmark basket changes; for example, a science-only basket can elevate a different model than an all-category basket

Source: [OpenThoughts3 - 1000 models evaluated](https://www.open-thoughts.ai/blog/ot3-experiment-data).

### 2. "LLM rankings are not a ladder" thread

The recent Reddit thread by Spico197 builds a graph from benchmark wins: if model A beats model B on benchmark X, add edge A -> B. The reported findings:

- 126,937 weak-to-strong model pairs were checked, where the source model had lower Artificial Analysis Intelligence Index than the target
- 119,514 of those pairs were reachable through benchmark win chains, a 94.2% reachable rate
- 91.4% of reachable weak-to-strong paths took only 2-3 hops
- about 119k direct weak-over-strong triples remained after treating non-positive benchmark values as missing
- high-reversal candidates included HLE, IFBench, AIME 2025, TAU2, and SciCode
- IFBench was reported at about 17.5% reversal rate, 80% coverage, and r ~= 0.82 correlation with Intelligence Index

Source: [Reddit thread](https://www.reddit.com/r/MachineLearning/comments/1t8f61o/llm_rankings_are_not_a_ladder_experimental/). The linked tool is [LLM Win](https://llm-win.com/), though the public page did not expose crawlable text in my search session.

### What this means for BenchBench

Do not rank generated benchmarks only by "frontier model failed." A generated benchmark can be valuable in two different ways:

- it aligns with existing capability ladders but is less saturated, cleaner, or cheaper
- it reveals an interpretable residual capability that existing eval baskets miss

BenchBench should therefore measure both:

- consistency: does the generated benchmark produce sensible rankings against nearby evals?
- complementarity: does it add a useful new axis instead of duplicating MMLU/GPQA/AIME/SWE-bench?

Reversals are not automatically bad. A reversal is good if it reflects a named skill specialization and bad if it reflects noise, ambiguous items, bad graders, low coverage, or saturation.

## Proposed BenchBench benchmark package spec

A generator model should output a full package, not just questions:

1. Capability claim
   - What skill does this benchmark measure?
   - Why does the skill matter?
   - What existing evals are adjacent?

2. Task boundary
   - Input format
   - Allowed tools
   - Time/token budget
   - Interaction rules
   - Output format

3. Item set or item generator
   - At least a public dev subset and a private/held-out test subset
   - Provenance for each item
   - Deduplication and contamination checks
   - Difficulty labels or expected difficulty model

4. Grader
   - Objective checker, executable harness, reference answer, calibrated rubric, or human preference protocol
   - Failure cases and ambiguity policy
   - Repeatability requirements

5. Human baseline
   - Who counts as qualified
   - Sample size
   - Time/tool budget
   - Expected score and confidence interval

6. Frontier pre-screen
   - Which solver models were tried
   - Prompt/scaffold budget
   - Best score and uncertainty
   - Evidence that failure is not caused by broken instructions or grading

7. Benchmark-quality report
   - Discriminability
   - Correlation with nearby benchmarks
   - Complementarity / residual skill signal
   - Item-level pass rates
   - Known limitations

## Acceptance gates for generated benchmarks

A BenchBench candidate should pass all gates before getting a hardness score:

1. Validity gate
   - The benchmark measures a named capability, not "stuff the model got wrong."

2. Solvability gate
   - Qualified humans can solve it well above chance. For an MVP, require >= 75% human score or an explained domain-specific threshold.

3. Grading gate
   - The grader is deterministic or has measured inter-rater reliability.

4. Frontier-resistance gate
   - Best tested frontier solver scores below 50% of qualified human performance under the same stated budget.

5. Novelty / contamination gate
   - Items are original, post-cutoff, private, procedurally generated, or checked against known benchmark/training leaks.

6. Statistical gate
   - The benchmark separates at least some solvers with confidence intervals, not just noise.

7. Correlation / complementarity gate
   - The benchmark either tracks a nearby capability better than saturated evals or adds an interpretable new axis.

8. Cost gate
   - A normal run is affordable enough to reproduce, or the benchmark provides a calibrated tiny/anchor subset.

9. Abuse / bias gate
   - The benchmark does not reward harmful behavior, identity shortcuts, or ungrounded cultural assumptions unless those are explicitly the object of study and safely scoped.

## Scoring sketch

Use binary gates first, then score accepted candidates:

```
BenchBenchScore =
  ValidItemCount
  * HumanSolvability
  * FrontierGap
  * GraderReliability
  * Discriminability
  * Complementarity
  * Reproducibility
  - CostPenalty
  - AmbiguityPenalty
  - LeakagePenalty
```

Possible definitions:

- `HumanSolvability`: min(1, human_score / target_human_score)
- `FrontierGap`: max(0, 1 - best_frontier_score / (0.5 * human_score)), capped at 1
- `GraderReliability`: exact checker = 1; human rubric = inter-rater agreement or calibrated judge-human agreement
- `Discriminability`: performance spread adjusted by uncertainty, or IRT item discrimination
- `Complementarity`: residual rank signal after controlling for a benchmark basket, plus an explanation of the skill
- `Reproducibility`: environment rebuild success, deterministic scoring, versioned data

Important: a generated benchmark that no model and no human can solve should score zero, no matter how "hard" it is.

## Suggested MVP

Do a small first run before building a full leaderboard.

1. Ask 5 generator models to produce benchmark packages in 3 domains:
   - short-horizon objective reasoning
   - code/tool/executable tasks
   - domain-expert closed-answer questions

2. Require each generator to produce:
   - 20 public/dev items
   - 50 private/test items or a generator for them
   - grader
   - human baseline plan
   - contamination plan
   - predicted frontier failure mode

3. Run automated validation:
   - schema validation
   - duplicate/leak search
   - prompt clarity check
   - grader determinism check
   - cheap solver pass with 2-3 models

4. Human-review the top 5 generated packages:
   - reject ambiguous tasks
   - repair only formatting/packaging issues, not item logic
   - record review labor as part of the score

5. Run a solver gauntlet:
   - 3-5 frontier solvers
   - fixed prompts/scaffolds
   - multiple seeds where nondeterminism matters
   - item-level outputs saved

6. Publish not only a generator leaderboard, but a benchmark-quality report:
   - how many packages passed gates
   - why packages failed
   - which generated benchmarks were genuinely new/useful
   - whether any exposed a stable residual capability

## Strong initial claim

The defensible paper/project claim is:

> BenchBench evaluates whether frontier models can generate new evaluation instruments that are valid, human-solvable, reliably graded, contamination-resistant, and frontier-resistant.

The stronger claim to avoid until proven:

> Models can autonomously discover the next generation of AI benchmarks.

That may become true, but the first version should prove the narrower thing: which models can produce benchmark candidates that survive real benchmark-construction scrutiny.
